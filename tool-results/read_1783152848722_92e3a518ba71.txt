   100→
   101→#### 🔴 #1 — Your build command can silently delete your database
   102→
   103→**In plain language:** Your app's "build" step (the command that runs every time you deploy to production) includes this:
   104→```
   105→prisma db push --accept-data-loss
   106→```
   107→`db push` is a tool meant for quick prototyping — it force-matches the database to your code's schema. `--accept-data-loss` literally means *"if you need to drop a column or a table to make them match, go ahead and drop it without asking."* Your own schema file has a comment admitting this already bit you: it says these admin tables must be copied into the main app's schema *"Otherwise, when the main app runs `prisma db push` during Vercel builds, it drops these tables — wiping all admin accounts on every deploy."*
   108→
   109→**Why it's dangerous:** On a database heading toward billions of rows, one bad schema comparison during a deploy can lock or destroy a table full of merchants' ledgers. There's no undo. This is the single most likely way you lose customer data catastrophically — and losing a shopkeeper's khata data even once ends your reputation.
   110→
   111→**The fix (do this first, it's not hard):** Switch to *migrations*. Instead of `db push`, use `prisma migrate` — it keeps a versioned history, reviews each change, and never silently drops data. In your build, run `prisma migrate deploy`. Locally, use `prisma migrate dev` to author changes. And turn on point-in-time backups on your database (Neon supports this) and take a snapshot *before* your first migration. Once `db push` is gone, the whole "copy admin tables into the Pro schema" hack can also go away.
   112→
   113→---
   114→
   115→#### 🔴 #2 — Money is stored as "Float" — you *will* get rupee/paise errors
   116→
   117→**In plain language:** In your database schema, every money field — prices, totals, GST, balances, `mrr` — is stored as a `Float`. A Float is a computer's approximate decimal. Computers can't store `0.1 + 0.2` exactly (they get `0.30000000000000004`). For most apps that's invisible. **For a bookkeeping app, it's fatal** — because your entire promise is "the numbers are correct."
   118→
   119→It's not theoretical in your code. When you calculate GST, you do `itemGst / 2` to split into CGST and SGST (in `transactions/route.ts`). Halving a float produces things like `9.000000000000002`. Multiply that across millions of transactions and a shopkeeper's khata drifts off by paise, then rupees. The moment a merchant notices their balance is "wrong by ₹0.03," they stop trusting the whole app.
   120→
   121→**The fix:** Store money as whole **paise** using a `BigInt` (so ₹1,234.56 is stored as the integer `123456`), or use Prisma's `Decimal` type. Do all math on the whole numbers, and only convert to rupees when displaying. Put every rupee↔paise conversion in one small file so it's done consistently. Since you have some data already, do it as a careful migration (add new columns, copy the values over rounded correctly, switch to the new columns, then remove the old ones).
   122→
   123→---
   124→
   125→#### 🔴 #3 — Your rate limits and AI quotas don't actually work in production
   126→
   127→**In plain language:** You added rate limiting (good instinct!) — limits like "20 AI scans per day" and "10 logins per minute." But they're stored *in the memory of one server*. Your app runs on Vercel, which spins up *many* copies of your server at once under load. Each copy has its own separate memory, so each copy counts from zero. Your own code comment admits it: *"For a startup app with <1000 users, in-memory is fine."*
   128→
   129→**Why it matters for you specifically:** Every AI scan calls a paid Gemini/OpenAI API. If your "50 scans/day" limit is really "50 per server copy, and there are 20 copies," someone can run 1,000 scans and you pay for all of them. At your target scale this is a real money leak. And when you add phone OTP login later, this same flaw becomes "SMS pumping" — bots triggering thousands of OTP texts that you get billed for.
   130→
   131→**The fix:** Move the limits to a shared memory that *all* server copies see — the standard tool is Redis (Upstash has a free tier and a drop-in library, `@upstash/ratelimit`). Your monthly free-tier counts are already stored in the database (good); it's the per-IP and per-day limits that need Redis. Small change, closes a real hole.

   133→---
   134→
   135→#### 🔴 #4 — At 1 lakh concurrent users, your database connections run out and everything crashes
   136→
   137→**In plain language:** Every copy of your server opens its own set of connections to Postgres. Postgres can only handle a few hundred connections total. Under a big traffic spike, Vercel launches hundreds of server copies in seconds, they all try to connect, Postgres says "no more room," and then *every request fails* — including the health checks — so the platform launches even more copies to compensate, which makes it worse. This is the classic serverless-database death spiral, and your setup (`DATABASE_URL` pointing straight at Neon with no pooling settings) walks right into it.
   138→
   139→**The fix:** Put a "connection pooler" between your app and the database — it's like a receptionist that manages a small pool of real connections and shares them across all the server copies. Neon has a built-in pooled endpoint; point your `DATABASE_URL` at it with `?pgbouncer=true&connection_limit=1`, and keep a separate direct URL only for running migrations. Also set query timeouts so a slow query fails fast instead of piling up. (Full detail in the technical appendix.)

   141→---
   142→
   143→#### 🔴 #5 — Heavy AI work blocks the request and can time out
   144→
   145→**In plain language:** When a user scans a bill, your code calls the AI and *waits* for the answer inside the same web request. A big handwritten image can take several seconds. On serverless there's a time limit, and none of your API routes set one (`maxDuration` isn't configured anywhere). So under load, slow scans can hit the platform timeout and fail with an error, and there's no "try again in the background" system.
   146→
   147→**The fix:** For anything slow — AI scanning, report PDFs, bulk imports — hand the work to a background "worker" and immediately tell the user "we're processing, we'll notify you." The standard tools are a job queue (BullMQ, or Upstash QStash which is serverless-friendly). At minimum, set an explicit time limit on each heavy route so failures are clean. (Detail in appendix.)

   151→#### 🟠 #6 — Smaller but real code issues
   152→
   153→These won't crash you tomorrow, but fix them in the first month:
   154→
   155→- **Payment verify isn't idempotent.** If the "confirm my payment" call runs twice (double-tap, network retry), you create two subscription records and extend the user's plan twice (`payment/verify/route.ts`). Fix: add a unique constraint on the payment ID (you already build the record ID from it, so this is nearly free) and wrap the writes in a transaction so they succeed or fail together.
   156→- **GST interstate split trusts the client.** Whether a sale is CGST+SGST (same state) or IGST (different state) is decided by a flag the phone sends, not calculated from the shop's state vs the customer's state. A wrong flag = a wrong GST return for that merchant. Fix: calculate it on the server from the two state codes.
   157→- **Some list endpoints have no size cap.** `/api/transactions?limit=99999999` will try to load the whole table into memory and crash the function. Cap every list at a sane maximum (e.g. 200) and use "cursor pagination" for big lists.
   158→- **Prisma logs every query in production** (`lib/db.ts` has `log: ['query']`). At scale that's expensive and it writes customers' personal data into your logs. Set it to `['error','warn']` in production.
   159→- **Party balances are recomputed in JavaScript on every read** (`parties/route.ts`) — correct, but slow at scale. Move to a database SUM query.

   163→## Part 4 — Security (the part V3 scored 7/10 and shouldn't have)
   164→
   165→Your V2/V3 reports gave Security a 7/10 and listed generic gaps (no encryption at rest, no rate limiting on AI). Those are real, but they missed the sharper ones — mostly in the **admin panel**, which is your highest-value target because one admin compromise exposes *every* merchant's books at once. Here's what I actually found.
   166→
   167→### 🔴 The admin panel has a "why did my login fail?" endpoint that helps attackers
   168→
   169→There's an endpoint `/api/admin/login-debug` that anyone can call without logging in, and it tells you *exactly* why a login failed: "this email isn't an admin," or "no account exists," or "**password is incorrect**," or "2FA required." For an attacker this is a gift — it confirms which emails are admins, whether they've set passwords, and whether 2FA is on, and it runs a real password check on whatever they submit. **Delete this endpoint (and any `*-debug` endpoint) before launch.** Debug tools should never ship to production.
   170→
   171→### 🔴 The admin "run any SQL" tool is guarded by a keyword blocklist (bypassable)
   172→
   173→Your admin panel lets a logged-in admin type raw SQL and run it (`database/query` → `executeSafeQuery`). To keep it "read-only," it checks the query text against a list of banned words (DROP, DELETE, etc.) and runs it with `$queryRawUnsafe`. Blocklists like this are famously easy to slip past, and this query runs against the *whole* database — every tenant. If an admin account is ever compromised, this is a master key to all merchant data.
   174→
   175→**The fix:** Don't rely on word-matching. Create a separate database login that has *only* read permission, and run these admin queries through it — then the *database itself* enforces read-only, no matter what text gets typed. Keep the row limit and timeout you already have, keep logging every query (you do — good), and require 2FA to use the feature. Better still, replace free-typed SQL with a fixed set of safe, pre-written report queries.
   176→
   177→### 🔴 Your login secret has a hardcoded fallback
   178→
   179→In `lib/auth.ts`, if the `NEXTAUTH_SECRET` environment variable is ever missing, the code falls back to a fixed string that's *in your source code*: `'ekbook-dev-secret-change-in-production'`. If that ever happens in production, anyone who's seen your code can forge a login token for any user. **Fix:** make the app refuse to start if the secret is missing, instead of using a fallback. One line.
   180→
   181→### 🟠 Other security items
   182→
   183→- **Sessions last 30 days as a self-contained token with no way to revoke them.** If someone steals a token, it works for a month and you can't kill it. For a financial app, shorten it and add a revocation path. (Detail in appendix.)
   184→- **Admin CSRF check allows requests with a missing Origin header** (`admin/middleware.ts`) — it only blocks when Origin is present and wrong. Prefer requiring a valid same-origin header on admin mutations.
   185→- **2FA exists in the admin schema but I couldn't confirm it's *enforced*** as a hard gate (the debug endpoint treats it as an informational message). Given what the admin panel can do — read every ledger, impersonate users, run SQL, deliver lending leads — make TOTP 2FA mandatory for all admin logins, and confirm the admin panel is on a separate domain/cookie from the Pro app.
   186→- **Password minimum is 6 characters** — raise to 8+ and ideally check against known-breached passwords.
   187→- **The two "encryption / monitoring" gaps from your V3 report:** Sentry is now done (credit you). Encryption-at-rest: turn on your database provider's disk encryption (baseline), and add field-level encryption specifically for the most sensitive fields (bank details, and any credit data if the lending feature ever ships). This matters for DPDP (Part 5).

   191→## Part 5 — The Legal Landmine Your Other Reports Completely Missed 🔴🔴
   192→
   193→**This is the most important section in this document. It is bigger than any bug.** Your V2/V3 reports mention "embedded finance / lending to shopkeepers" as an *opportunity* (🟢 Massive) and never once flag it as a risk. But your admin panel already contains a **built, revenue-booking lending pipeline** — and as written, it's a serious regulatory violation waiting to happen.
   194→
   195→### What your code actually does
   196→
   197→In the admin panel (`lib/lending-pipeline.ts`, `lib/credit-score.ts`, `data-monetization/*`, `lending-pipeline/deliver`), the system:
   198→1. Calculates a **credit score** for every merchant from their bookkeeping data (their sales, how reliably their customers pay, etc.),
   199→2. Sorts merchants into bands (excellent / good / fair / poor),
   200→3. **Sends each merchant's financial profile to NBFC "partners" via webhook** as a sales "lead," and
   201→4. **Books revenue per lead** — ₹200 for excellent, ₹150 good, ₹100 fair (hardcoded in the file).
   202→
   203→Your own code gives away the problem. The privacy comment in the pipeline file literally says: *"DPDP compliance: user consent required (future: consent flag on User model)."* Translation: **the code ships and counts revenue today; the consent it legally requires is a to-do for later.**
   204→
   205→### Why this can shut you down (in plain language)
   206→
   207→Two laws, both currently in force:
   208→
   209→**1. The DPDP Act (India's data-protection law).** You're taking merchants' personal and financial data — and, worse, data derived from their *customers'* payment behaviour (people who never even signed up with you) — and shipping it to third-party lenders for a brand-new purpose (making money on loan leads). Under DPDP you need each person's clear, specific, informed, prior consent for exactly that purpose, with a record of it. Your schema doesn't even have a consent field yet. Penalties run up to ₹250 crore per violation category. This is precisely the kind of processing that law was written to punish.
   210→
   211→**2. RBI's Digital Lending Directions, 2025** (note: your instinct to think about the "2022 guidelines" is out of date — they were replaced in May 2025). The moment you hand borrower leads to an NBFC for a fee, you legally become a "Lending Service Provider" and your app becomes a "Digital Lending App." That triggers a whole regime: a formal contract with the NBFC, the NBFC must register your app with RBI, loan money must flow *directly* between the borrower's bank and the NBFC (never through any account you control), you can't charge the borrower, and the NBFC is on the hook for everything you do (so they'll audit you hard before signing). A per-lead "data monetization" model that isn't structured through a proper NBFC arrangement risks being treated as unregulated lending facilitation or data-broking.
   212→
   213→### What to do — pick one, before you launch
   214→
   215→**Option A — safest, and what I'd do: turn the whole pipeline off for launch.**
   216→Feature-flag off the credit-scoring, data-monetization, and lending-delivery features. Ship BahiKhata as a pure, clean bookkeeping app. This deletes your single biggest existential risk in one commit. Add lending *later*, properly, as its own project. (This is exactly the Khatabook path — win on bookkeeping first, monetize on lending once you can do it compliantly.)
   217→
   218→**Option B — if lending is central to your plan: build it correctly, which is a real project, not a patch.**
   219→- Add genuine, specific, opt-in consent in the *merchant's* app before any data leaves — a real "I agree to share my business data with lending partners" screen, unbundled from your terms, revocable, with a stored record.
   220→- Only ever send data for merchants who actively opted in, and only their *own* business data — never their customers' behaviour without a separate legal basis.
   221→- Sign a proper NBFC partnership (RE–LSP contract), let the NBFC register the app with RBI, and make sure loan money never touches any account you control.
   222→- Get a fintech lawyer to review the structure *before* one real lead is sent.
   223→
   224→Until Option A or B is done, **do not enable this pipeline in production.** I want to be very direct: this is the thing most likely to end the company, and it's the thing your other reports were most positive about. That gap is why "cheerleader" reports are dangerous.
   225→
   226→### The other regulatory homework (from my earlier analysis, in plain language)
   227→
   228→You're launching a fintech in India. Beyond the lending issue, you'll need:
   229→- **Incorporate a Private Limited company** before launch (every other item below assumes one, and it protects you personally if a merchant sues over a data issue).
   230→- **DPDP basics even without lending:** a consent screen and privacy notice (in Hindi + English at minimum), a way for users to download and delete their data, a data-deletion process across all your systems, and a breach-response plan (India requires notifying the regulator within 72 hours and CERT-In within 6 hours for cyber incidents). The DPDP full-compliance deadline is 13 May 2027, but the regulator is already live — build it in now, don't retrofit.
